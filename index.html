Contextualizing American Health Inequality

Is the United States healthcare system truly exemplary? Or is this a widespread misconception? When asked, “Where does the greatest healthcare inequality exist”, people likely point to developing countries… However, as noted by experts in healthcare worldwide, healthcare inequality exists to a severe degree in the United States and often goes unnoticed. Public health scholars like Ichiro Kawachi go as far as saying,

“There are huge inequalities in this country that often get overlooked … If you want to observe the problems of poverty and inequality, you don’t need to travel all the way to Malawi. You can go to a rural house in America.”

Wealth and Health Care

Spending on health care has increased dramatically...

Over past few decades, there have been great advances in science and medicine that have lead to cures and treatments for many previously uncurable diseases. While this has gone a long way to improving the health of the country on a whole, increasing life expectancy and other metrics of health, it has created a new cause of health inequality: wealth. As you can see from the interactive charts to the right, spending on health care has increased at an incredible rate, and most of this increase is due to health insurance - specifically, private health insurance. This means that there now exists a large gap in life expectancy between wealthier people, who can afford good health insurance, and poorer ones who cannot.

Access to Health Care

Many Americans do not have regular access to proper health care due to their income level, state's health care facilities, or other social determinants.

Check out our comparison of health care access and outcomes between US states below.




According to the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality, proper health care access means that patients should be able to enter the health care system, gain access to medical provider sites, and have medical providers who meet their needs and build strong, trusting relationships. The scatterplot to the left shows how US states differ in their ability to provide enough hospitals and active physicians for their population and how this may impact the wait time for patients to see a doctor when in the emergency room. Click each state's bubble for more details!



“20.3 million U.S. children - 28% of the pediatric population under the age of 18 - are without adequate health care.” - Children's Health Fund


Unfortunately, all states within the US do not provide adequate levels of health care access to all of its residents. This can have a great negative impact for ultimate health care outcomes, contributing to the ultimate health inequalities we see within the United States. Check out the quartile-ranking of the US states for health care access metrics (percentage of adults who don't have a regular doctor & percentage of uninsured adults), and how this corresponds to health care outcome metrics (infant death rate, patients' reports of their own health).
